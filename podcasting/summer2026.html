Summer 2026, House Made of Dawn: Art by Native Americans 1880 to Now, Selections from the Hsu-Tang Collection

This midwinter recess, students explored the House Made of Dawn: Art by Native Americans 1880 to Now, Selections from the Hsu-Tang Collection exhibition. Each episode delves into the history art, artists, or themes featured in the exhibit.

No More Stolen Sisters: an Investigation of the Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women Movement

Hosts: Jian, Harper, Abby, Sofia| Sources

In this episode of Through Fresh Eyes: Your podcast for history through a teen perspective, we explore the art piece “No More Stolen Sisters” created in 2025, through a critical and interpretive lens. This piece comes from A House Made of Dawn, an exhibit highlighting indigenous art from the 1880s onward which is being housed in the New York Historical for the 250th celebration of US independence. We were particularly drawn to this piece because of its bold lettering and stark red hand, which symbolized the silencing of indigenous female voices.

We use this piece to explore the power of “artivism”, or art activism, and delve into the MMIW or Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women crisis and movement, while exploring the trials and triumphs of community organizing. It is often easy to only victimize these indigenous women and focus on the harm inflicted. However, in this episode we aim to highlight the activism and strength of indigenous people and artists to bring attention to this long persisting crisis. The MMIW crisis is not only an issue of the present, but one rooted in colonial history, which we begin to explore in this episode.

As youth who had not heard of the MMIW crisis, we wanted to highlight this piece to bring attention to the critical stories which too often go untold and overlooked.

Through Fresh Eyes: A Peace of Medal

Hosts: Georgina, Raina, Oscar, James, and Ethan| Sources

This episode of the New York Historical’s “Through Fresh Eyes” podcast is entitled “A Peace of Medal”. This episode discusses the history of peace medals in America through the perspective of teens. Peace medals began as early as 1693 with the French ruler, King Louis XIV. This tradition was later adopted by the US government and presidents would often distribute the medals to indigenous tribes to develop and nurture diplomatic relations with them. However, as time went on, many of the land treaties that accompanied these medals were broken and the Native American’s trust in the US government shattered as the US kept wanting to take more and more of their land.

This episode describes these complicated feelings through analyzing the painting, “But We Wear Peace Medals” by Valjean McCarty Hessing, a contemporary Native American artist. This piece is currently on display at the New York Historical in the exhibition “House Made of Dawn”. In this episode, a thorough history of these peace medals will be given, as well as an in-depth artistic analysis on “But We Wear Peace Medals”. Through analyzing history with a modern lens, there will also be a discussion on the emotional impact of this painting and peace medals. We hope that listeners will find this episode meaningful through hearing new ideas and perspectives as well as highlighting indigenous art and artists.

Revisiting the End

Hosts: Pompie, Subah, Carolina, Thomas, Marie| Sources

In this episode we talk about Joy Harjo’s book of poems, How We Became Human, specifically her poem When the World Ended as we Knew it. It goes over topics such as 9/11, colonization, the end of the world, and resilience after disaster.

We dissect this poem bit by bit, going through the historical context, give our interpretations, showcase interviews and give details and background for the poem. This includes a guest appearance from David Hearst Thomas, the curator of North American Archaeology at the American Museum of Natural History in New York, who worked in North American archeology for 54 years.

Joy Harjo also talks about the “end of the world.” What does she mean by this? She talks about that fireball which changed and ended the lives of so many as the end of the world, and in a sense to her, it was, and also the birth of a new one. We go deeply into the concept of the “end of the world,” analyze it.

Not Just a Shoe: White Man’s Moccasins

Hosts: Emma C., Julian B., Kai A., Max M., and Oona I. | Sources

In this episode of “Through Fresh Eyes”, Not Just a Shoe: White Man’s Moccasins explores Laguna Pueblo Lee Marmon’s photo “White Man’s Mocassins”, currently featured in the New York Historical’s exhibit House Made of Dawn: Art by Native Americans 1880 to Now.
Marmon was one of the first indigenous photographers to take photos of the indigenous community, and was incredibly influential in shifting the relationship between photography and Native communities. While previous photographers like English-Canadian Edward Curtis can be seen as exploiting native communities and perpetuating narratives of extinction, the modern sneakers and pose of “Old Man Jeff” serve as a striking reminder that Indigenous communities are still present and strong.Marmon also powerfully aimed to give his subjects autonomy in defining themselves, whereas historically many marginalized communities have been defined by their colonial oppressors. We delve into how Marmon’s photo challenges traditionally held stereotypes in the indigenous community, and how photographers and advocates today carry on his legacy.

More Than a Sunset

Hosts: Emma, Chloe, Sharika, Quentin, Amadeus | Sources

In this episode of Through Fresh Eyes, we explore how a single work of art reveals the lasting impact of Native American boarding schools and the resilience of the children within them. With first impressions, historical context, survivor testimony, and analysis of the painting,we are driven by the question: How do you preserve your identity when it’s being stripped away?

Together we examine the meaning behind the boy’s identical uniform, the distant boarding school, and the smudging ceremony occurring. We also focus on the colorful sunset framing this scene. Instead of focusing on the pain and hardships, this painting shows the small acts of resistance that allow Indigenous culture and traditions to remain despite the attempts to erase it.

By the end of the episode, you will learn more about wicihitowin and how there’s more than what meets the eye. It shows us that identity can be preserved through community and culture even when facing hardship. Because sometimes the quiet acts of resistance are the most powerful.
